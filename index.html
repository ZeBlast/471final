What do graduates earn after college?

Where you go to college shapes how much debt you carry for decades. But tuition sticker prices hide the real story. This tool combines College Scorecard cost and debt data with Post-Secondary Employment Outcomes earnings data to reveal something most rankings never show: which states, regions, and types of schools actually produce graduates who can afford the debt they take on. The findings are surprising, and in some cases alarming.

The Story

What the data actually tells us

Before you explore, here is what we found by analyzing every school in this dataset. These are real conclusions drawn from the data, not assumptions.

Finding 1: The New York Anomaly

New York graduates carry the least debt relative to what they earn, by a wide margin

Among all states with at least three schools in the 2019 cohort, New York ranks first in earnings-to-debt ratio at 3.54x. The driver is the CUNY system. Schools like Baruch College, Lehman College, and Hunter College charge roughly $7,000 per year in tuition, yet their graduates earn $43,000 to $54,000 in year one with completer debt as low as $10,500. Baruch graduates earn $53,849 with just $11,130 in debt, a ratio of 4.84x.

This is not because New York graduates earn vastly more. Their median year-1 earnings of $44,632 are close to the national median. The difference is the debt side: CUNY schools hold debt so low that graduates are almost immediately financially stable. The lesson is clear: the price of school matters more than its prestige.

Try it: filter the map to NY and color by earnings-to-debt ratio.

Finding 2: The South Carolina Problem

South Carolina sits at the bottom of the debt efficiency table, and the gap is severe

South Carolina ranks last among states with sufficient data, with a median earnings-to-debt ratio of just 1.59x. Several schools in the state graduate students who owe more than they can comfortably repay. Allen University graduates earn $26,970 in year one but carry $35,000 in debt, a ratio of 0.77x, meaning their debt actually exceeds a full year of earnings. Morris College and South Carolina State University sit at 1.02x and 1.06x respectively.

Many of these are HBCUs serving students with high Pell grant rates, meaning they are already among the most financially vulnerable students. The data suggests these schools need more support to reduce the debt burden on the students who can least afford it.

Try it: filter the map to SC and color by completer debt.

Finding 3: Low Tuition Schools Win on Efficiency

Schools charging under $6,000 per year produce graduates with nearly half the debt of expensive schools

When we split schools into tuition bands, a striking pattern emerges. Schools with in-state tuition under $6,000 produce graduates with median completer debt of just $16,206, compared to $25,908 at schools charging over $20,000. Yet year-1 earnings are nearly identical: $44,924 versus $44,354.

Paying more does not get you more. The earnings-to-debt ratio at low-tuition schools reaches 3.0x, versus 1.8x at high-tuition schools. Students who attend affordable public colleges and state systems are starting their careers from a fundamentally stronger financial position, often for the same salary.

Try it: sort states by earnings-to-debt ratio in Section 1, then compare to total cost.

Finding 4: A Slow but Real Improvement

Nationally, the debt-to-earnings burden has improved from 2001 to 2019

Looking across graduation cohorts from 2001 to 2019, median completer debt has stayed remarkably flat, hovering around $21,700 to $22,000 throughout. Meanwhile, median year-1 earnings climbed from $42,152 in 2001 to $45,123 in 2019. That pushed the earnings-to-debt ratio from 1.90x to 2.13x.

The narrative that student debt has spiraled out of control across all sectors is not fully supported by PSEO outcomes data for bachelor's graduates. The crisis is concentrated in specific institutions and states, not evenly distributed. The 2010 cohort saw the worst ratio at 1.86x, corresponding with post-recession job market weakness, but outcomes recovered and have improved since.
